Question 1

Describe a software challenge you recently faced, and how you solved it.

The project talked about in this question can be viewed from the Data Structures report.

I was working on a project where I designed a process scheduler using whatever algorithm I desired, so long as I had prioritising tasks set to one of three levels on initialisation. These values could later be changed though, in order to avoid task stagnation if required. I used priority scheduling as a base, using an aging system alongside that added points to processes that have not yet been completed. When a process was initialised, its age was tracked until it had been fully processed. When its age was greater than a set limit, its points total meant it had reached the top of the priority queue. This limit was not set in stone; rather it was calculated by the average burst time of the completed processes.

This meant that whilst I was not able to use actual bursts in the calculations, I was able to use an estimate based on the real data that was being passed through the scheduler. The more processes that arrived in the system, the more data it had to work out what the average burst time is, and therefore the more efficient the scheduler became. This was particularly helpful in working out the order of processes when longer processes entered the system. If a process was taking longer than the average burst time, the system knew that it must be very close to competition and therefore it continued to be processed for the final few bursts, rather than switching to a new process.

This prevented long process starvation, as slightly longer processes were allowed to finish being processed, when previously they would not have with default process scheduling. These solutions aided me in decreasing the average waiting and turnaround time, resulting in more processes being completed quicker than they were before. Whilst the brief only required hard coding 5 processes into the system as it was mainly testing my algorithm application, the way it works would allow it to be expanded to an infinite number of processes. Therefore, if I were to reattempt this task in the future, I would change the processes to be dynamic rather than only allowing 5 processes in the final code.

Question 2

Describe a software project that you worked on, and how you organised it.

The project talked about in this question can be viewed from the Agile Management report.

I have worked on various software projects ranging between 1 to 6 people on the team. My largest project in terms of deliverables is an application prototype that I developed in university for a client in a team of 3. The client was a biology teacher who wanted a fun and interactive way of teaching one of their modules to their KS3 students (Years 7 to 9), demonstrated through a short playable demo.

The project went through all the stages from conception to the completion of a playable demo, which was ready to be uploaded and used as a first introduction level. This was managed through many agile methodologies, such as scrums and sprints, allowing my team consistent interaction and feedback with the client. Each person in the team was allocated set tasks based on their skillset, decided as a team in the sprint meetings, in order to plan and organise the work and judge our weekly progress.

Each week, I arranged a sprint meeting with my team and the client to discuss various topics of the week. Towards the beginning of the project, this included testing relevant competition in the market, creating a list of our technical specifications, as well as a usability test plan. As time went on, we started drafting the initial UI, developing a storyboard, and utilising our previous market research to help us in choosing our art style. In the following sprints, we developed use case diagrams, produced high-level design architecture, and monitored accessibility considerations that could affect product usability.

As each sprint went by, my team and I would discuss with the client how each stage of the project was developing. For example, our draft UI turned into a more defined UI, and eventually a finalised UI. Once all of the elements of our project had gone through the process of finalisation, me and my team tested the application with our audience of KS3 students. This allowed us to make final tweaks, alongside the general debugging of any potential code issues, before finally submitting our created demo.
